There are two ways people check their money, and both are broken. The first is compulsive: refreshing the banking app, watching the balance twitch, feeling a little worse each time and learning nothing. The second is avoidance: not looking at all until a card gets declined or a bill lands wrong. Neither tells you anything useful, and both feel bad.

There's a third way, and it takes about ten minutes a month. This is the routine Pare is built around, and you can run it in any finance app — but it's worth explaining why *monthly* is the right cadence and what ten minutes should actually cover.

## Why monthly beats daily

Daily balance-checking feels responsible. It isn't. A single day's balance is noise — it swings with the timing of a paycheck, a rent payment, a card statement clearing. Watching it move teaches you nothing about whether your spending is actually fine, and the anxiety it generates has no outlet, because there's no decision you make daily that a balance should drive.

Your financial life runs on a monthly clock. Rent, salary, most bills, and your statements all arrive monthly. So the honest unit of review is a month. Look once, after the month has closed enough to be legible, and you see the signal instead of the noise.

This lines up with how statement-based tools work anyway. Statements lag the calendar — a statement closes, then shows up a few days later — so the current month is always a little incomplete. That's not a limitation to fight; it's a reminder that the useful review happens on last month's finished data, not today's half-formed total.

## The routine, minute by minute

Here's the ten minutes. Each step maps to something Pare puts on one screen, but the questions are what matter — you're looking for a small number of answers, not admiring charts.

### Minutes 1–2: Did you spend less than you made?

Start with the one number that matters most: net cash flow. Income minus everything that went out. If it's positive, last month you got a little richer; if it's negative, a little poorer. That's the whole game, and most people never look at it directly. Pare shows income vs. spend and the net for the month up top — read it, note whether it's green or red, move on.

### Minutes 3–4: What moved, and why?

Now the category breakdown. You're not auditing every line — you're scanning for what *changed* versus a normal month. Dining doubled? Travel showed up? A category you don't recognize crept up? Pare sorts categories and flags month-over-month moves, so the outliers surface on their own. Spend your attention only on the one or two categories that jumped.

### Minutes 5–6: Any subscription surprises?

Recurring charges are where money leaks quietly. Check the subscription view for anything new, anything you forgot you were paying for, and — this is the sneaky one — anything charged twice or that crept up in price. Pare detects recurring merchants and flags double-bills and variable amounts. Cancel one forgotten subscription a year and this step pays for the other nine minutes forever.

### Minutes 7–8: Was it a trend or a one-off?

A single big month can scare you for no reason. Maybe you bought a flight or a fridge. Before you conclude you're overspending, separate the one-time charges from your real rhythm. Pare has a "baseline" view that strips out large one-off charges so you can see your true month-to-month discretionary spending. If the scary total was one appliance, you can relax. If it's your baseline creeping up, that's worth acting on.

### Minutes 9–10: Are you on track for next month?

End by looking forward, briefly. Glance at the cash-flow forecast and any spending goals you've set. Pare projects your balance 30, 60, and 90 days out from your latest reconciled figure and shows how each category is pacing against its limit. You're not making a budget here — you're just checking there's no cliff coming and no goal already blown. Ten minutes, done.

## The honest limit of a monthly cadence

Here's the trade-off, stated plainly: a monthly review will not catch a fraudulent charge the day it happens. If someone skims your card on the 3rd, you might not see it in Pare until the statement closes and you upload it weeks later.

So don't ask a monthly review to be a fraud monitor. Keep your bank's own transaction alerts turned on for that — they're free, instant, and better at it than any third-party tool. The monthly review is for understanding and steering your money; real-time alerts are for catching theft. Different jobs. Use both, and don't confuse one for the other. ## What to do with what you find

A review is only worth ten minutes if it occasionally changes something. Most months, it won't — you'll confirm you spent less than you made, nothing weird happened, and you'll close the tab. That's a good outcome, not a wasted review; the value was in *knowing*, not in acting.

But when a step does turn something up, act on it while it's in front of you, because a "note to self" almost never survives the week. Forgotten subscription? Cancel it in the same sitting. Category that quietly crept up two months running? Set a spending goal on it so next month's review flags it automatically. Forecast showing a dip before a big bill? Move a little to savings now instead of being surprised later. Small corrections, made immediately, are the entire return on the habit.

## The quarterly version

Once a quarter, give yourself twenty minutes instead of ten and zoom out. The monthly review is about the last month; the quarterly one is about the trend. Look at three months side by side: is your baseline spending drifting up? Is net cash flow consistently positive, or is it only positive in the months you get paid twice? Has your net worth actually moved? Quarterly is also the right cadence for the boring maintenance — pruning subscriptions you've stopped using, checking that your categorization rules still make sense, and downloading any statements you've let pile up. Monthly keeps you honest; quarterly keeps you pointed in the right direction.

## Make it a habit, not a chore

The trick to keeping this up is to make it small and scheduled. Pick a day — the first Sunday of the month, payday, whatever sticks — put it on the calendar for ten minutes, and don't let it grow. The moment a "review" becomes an hour of spreadsheet archaeology, you'll stop doing it. Ten minutes on the questions above, once a month, beats an anxious daily glance every single time, because it ends in understanding instead of dread.
