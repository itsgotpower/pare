Subscriptions are the easiest money to lose, because they're designed to be forgotten. Each one is small, it renews on its own, and the whole point of the pricing is that you don't notice it leave. Add up a couple of streaming services you stopped watching, a trial that quietly converted, and an app that raised its price 20% last spring, and you're often paying a few hundred dollars a year for things you'd cancel in a heartbeat if they were all on one screen. This is how to get them on one screen — in fifteen minutes, in whatever tool you use.

## The three ways subscriptions leak money

Before the steps, know what you're hunting for. Recurring charges go wrong in three specific ways:

- **The forgotten one.** You're paying for something you no longer use — the classic. Easy to cancel once you actually see it.
- **The one that crept up.** The charge you approved at $8 is now $13. You never re-approved it; the price just drifted, one small hike at a time.
- **The double-bill.** The same service charged twice in a month, or two overlapping subscriptions that do the same job — two music services, say, or a standalone app plus the bundle that quietly started including it. This one hides the best, because each charge looks normal on its own.

An audit is just looking for those three things on purpose.

## The 15-minute audit (works in any tool)

This works with nothing but your statements and a notepad. If you use an app that detects recurring charges, it does the finding for you — but the method is the same either way.

1. **Pull the last three months of statements.** You need a few months because a subscription is a *pattern* — one charge isn't enough to tell a subscription from a one-off. Do this part now, even before you've set aside the fifteen minutes; it's the step people skip.
2. **List every charge that repeats.** Same merchant, roughly the same amount, roughly monthly or annually. Write each one down once.
3. **Tag each with the three traps.** For every line: do I still use this? Did the price go up? Is anything here charged twice or overlapping with something else?
4. **Do the annual math.** A $13/month charge isn't a $13 decision — it's a $156/year decision. Multiply everything by twelve (or read the annual charge straight off) so you're deciding on the real number.
5. **Cancel the losers in the same sitting.** This is the whole return on the exercise, and it's the step that evaporates if you defer it. A "note to self, cancel later" almost never survives the week. If you found it, kill it now.

That's it. Most of the fifteen minutes is step two; the value is in step five.

One thing that trips people up: annual subscriptions. A yearly charge only shows up once, on its anniversary month, so a three-month window can miss it entirely. If you can pull a full year of statements for step one, do — the annual charges are often the biggest single line items and the easiest to forget, precisely because they bill so rarely.

## Actually cancelling: the part they make hard

Finding a subscription is the easy half; companies put real effort into the second half. A few things that make a cancellation actually stick:

- **Go straight for the cancel flow, not the settings maze.** Many services bury it — searching "[service] cancel subscription" and following the current steps is usually faster than hunting through the app.
- **Expect a retention offer, and decide before you see it.** A discount to stay is a genuine fork: take it only if you'd have kept the thing at that price anyway. "Half off for three months" on something you don't use is still money leaving.
- **Time annual cancellations right after a renewal, not before.** You keep what you paid for through the term and simply won't be charged again. Set a reminder for a week before the next renewal so it isn't a scramble.
- **Get the confirmation in writing.** A cancellation email or a screenshot is your proof if a charge shows up next month anyway — which happens more often than it should.

The tactics matter because an audit that ends at "I should cancel that" changes nothing. The one that ends at a confirmation email is the one that saves you money.

## The honest limits

Two caveats, because a subscription audit built on statements has real edges:

- **It sees charges after they clear, not the day of.** Statements lag the calendar, so a brand-new subscription won't show up until it's billed a couple of times and the statements arrive. This is a review tool, not a real-time tripwire.
- **Detection needs history.** A merchant has to charge you across a few months before a tool can be confident it's recurring and not a coincidence. Your first month in any app won't have much to flag; by month three it will.
